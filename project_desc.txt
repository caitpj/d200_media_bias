D200 Project
Stefan Bucher

The goal of the small-scale research project is to apply machine learning to an economic
question, and to permit you to develop your skills in an area of your interest. The format
is intended to simulate submitting to a research conference, and you should treat it as such:
focus on a clear research question, a sound method, and transparent communication.

Assessment and Deliverables
The research project is assessed via a written project report and an oral examination which
constitutes a brief presentation and a Question and Answer session. All elements are essential
and constitute 100% weighting.

Project Report (due 16 Mar 2026, 12 noon)
The project report should be of 3 single-spaced pages (approximately 1500 words) including
figures, and is to be submitted through the Faculty’s submission system.
A strong report is concise and easy to follow. The report should clearly articulate:
• Motivation and research question: What are you asking and why does it matter?
• Minimal literature review: What is already known that your project builds on?
• Data: What data are you using and how did you prepare it?
• Methodology: What ML technique(s) are you using and why?
• Implementation Details (important!): How exactly did you implement the ML technique?
Provide key code snippets or pseudocode if helpful.
• Results: What do you find? Include figures/tables where informative.
• Discussion: What worked? What did not work and why? What next steps would you
take?
To format your report I recommend using a style file for conference submission (NeurIPS,
ICML, etc.). When doing so, please be careful that the metadata (title, author, etc.) of your
submitted file is correct.

Oral Examination (23-25 Mar 2026)
The oral examination will last approximately 15 minutes per student, and will start with a
brief (5-7 minutes) presentation in which you are asked (much like in the report) to cover:
• research question
• brief description of dataset
• ML method (be precise!)
• preliminary results
• challenges & how they could be addressed with more time
Please prepare slides for the presentation and submit them before your examination at https://
classroom.github.com/classrooms/195107486-machine-learning-in-economics-2026 as part of a
GitHub repository containing all your code and data (or clear data access instructions) for the
project along with the final report and slides. Include a README with basic instructions allowing
me to reproduce your results.

Rubric
No specific rubric is provided, but marking will take into account the clarity with which you
address your chosen research question and your understanding of the methods used.

Advice
The most important advice by far is to focus on clarity: Keep it simple, but make it work. Allow
me to understand exactly what you did (and make sure you do, too!). Correct implementation
and clear explanation are more important than complexity.
Project Steps
1. Pick an economic question that ML can help answer.
2. Find & preprocess data.
3. Apply ML Methods, possibly compare with traditional econometric methods.

Inspiration
Upon popular request, below are some example ideas and projects from previous years.
Previous years’ projects covered a wide range of applied topics that can help you brainstorm:
• causal inference and treatment heterogeneity (DDML, causal forests, policy evaluation)
• macro nowcasting/forecasting (GDP, recession signals, alternative data like Google
Trends)
• platform economics (recommendation systems, churn prediction)
• labor/organization questions (employee attrition, wage premiums)
• housing and auto price prediction (house prices, used car values)
• auction and platform behavior (pay-to-bid auctions, pricing dynamics)
• mobility and energy demand modeling
• algorithmic fairness and discrimination (lending, pricing)
• physics-informed/quant methods (PINNs for Black–Scholes)
• financial time-series forecasting (stocks, gold, crypto; ML vs ARIMA/VAR)
• text-as-data and sentiment (parliamentary debates, news, social media)
• volatility and event detection in financial news (topic models, NLP classifiers)
• credit risk and default prediction (loan default, credit scoring, tabular deep learning)
• portfolio construction and hedging (deep PPP, credit curve hedging, PCA/K-means)
Please use them only as vague inspiration to get a sense of the types of projects that have
been done, but you are explicitly encouraged to come up with your own ideas and the above
are in no way exhaustive or representative.
